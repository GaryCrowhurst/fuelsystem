Fuel Injection Systems

Multi-Point Fuel Injection (MPFI)

How it works: In multi-point injection, there are TWO different methods of injector positioning. Each cylinder has its own injector that sprays fuel directly into the intake port. This provides precise fuel control for each cylinder.

‚úì Advantages

  • More precise fuel control
  • Better fuel economy
  • Lower emissions
  • Improved performance
  • Better cold starting

‚úó Disadvantages

  • More complex system
  • Higher cost
  • More components to fail
  • Requires electronic control

Single Point Injection (SPI)

How it works: A single injector is located in the throttle body, spraying fuel for all cylinders. The fuel mixes with air before being distributed to each cylinder through the intake manifold.

‚úì Advantages

  • Simpler design
  • Lower cost
  • Easier to maintain
  • Fewer components

‚úó Disadvantages

  • Less precise fuel control
  • Poorer fuel distribution
  • Lower efficiency
  • Higher emissions

Carburettor Systems

Simple Carburettor

Basic Operation: A simple single-jet carburettor is a basic operating system. The fuel is maintained at a level shown below. When the engine starts up, the pressure at atmospheric pressure is forced through the jet. This speeds up and reduces the pressure, creating suction that draws fuel from the jet.

Float and Needle Valve

The float maintains a constant fuel level in the chamber. As fuel is used, the float drops and opens the needle valve to allow more fuel in. When the correct level is reached, the float rises and closes the valve.

Main Jet

The jet is a precisely sized hole that meters the fuel flow. Fuel is drawn through the jet by the low pressure created in the venturi. The jet size controls the air-fuel mixture strength.

Venturi (Choke Tube)

The venturi is a narrowing in the air passage. As air speeds up through the restriction, it creates low pressure (Bernoulli's principle). This low pressure draws fuel from the jet.

Throttle Valve

The throttle valve controls the amount of air-fuel mixture entering the engine. When you press the accelerator, the throttle opens to allow more mixture through, increasing engine speed.

Constant Choke Carburettor

Construction: Named because the choke (venturi) size remains constant. The main parts include the float chamber, main jet, throttle valve, and fixed venturi. Base construction shows how it is made.

The constant choke carburettor maintains a fixed venturi size, but uses different jet systems for various engine operating conditions (idle, part-throttle, full throttle).

Constant Depression (Variable Venturi) Carburettor

How it Works: Complete the air flow shown, indicating how it flows through the carburettor. The piston position changes with the throttle valve, varying the venturi size to maintain constant depression (vacuum).

The drawing shows the base construction of a constant depression carburettor. As air flow increases, a piston rises to open the venturi more, keeping the air velocity and vacuum constant.

‚úì Key Feature

  • Piston moves to maintain constant vacuum
  • Tapered needle varies fuel flow
  • Self-adjusting for engine load
  • Smoother operation across rev range

üìù Operation

  • Air flow lifts the piston
  • Needle rises with piston
  • Larger needle area allows more fuel
  • Maintains correct mixture automatically

Why Modern Vehicles Use Fuel Injection

  • Emissions Regulations: Injection provides precise control needed to meet strict emission standards
  • Fuel Economy: Better fuel atomisation and distribution improves efficiency
  • Performance: More accurate fuelling across all engine speeds and loads
  • Reliability: Fewer mechanical parts to wear out or adjust
  • Altitude Adaptation: Sensors automatically compensate for air pressure changes
  • Diagnostics: Electronic systems can self-diagnose faults
